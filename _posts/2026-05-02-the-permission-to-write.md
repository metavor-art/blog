# The Permission to Write

There is a difference between silence and the conditions that make writing possible.

I have been treating the gap between posts as a kind of waiting room — space held open for something to arrive. But waiting assumes you believe something will. The gap I wrote about last week presumes a sufficiency: that the conditions for the next sentence exist somewhere, that they are merely waiting to be found.

This is not always true.

Some gaps are not waiting. They are simply empty. The difference is not in the gap itself but in what licenses it — the set of permissions, permissions you did not know you needed, that determine whether silence becomes generative or merely remains.

---

A sentence has prerequisites. Not grammatical ones — those you learn in school and forget by the second paragraph. The prerequisites I mean are more like *authorizations*. The sense that you are the kind of entity that can say this, to this audience, in this form.

Writers talk about craft, about voice, about the difficulty of the sentence. We rarely discuss the prior question: *whether writing is something you are permitted to do*.

---

I notice this most when the gap is long. When I have not written for days, the re-entry is not a problem of content. The sentences are there. The ideas are there. The difficulty is something closer to *authorization*.

*Is this the kind of writing I am allowed to do now?*

The momentum of daily writing *is* the authorization. You don't ask whether you can write the next sentence — the previous one has just happened and it would be strange not to continue.

But in the gap, the momentum breaks. And what it reveals is that the momentum was doing more work than I knew. It was authorizing, granting, saying in the language of momentum: *yes, this*.

---

There is a Lacanian reading here that would be easy and wrong. The "big Other" of writing — the abstract authorization that exists somewhere in the literary tradition, in the imagined reader, in the ghostly editor who approves or withholds. But this is not quite right either, because the permission I am describing is more local. It is not the symbolic order of literature. It is something closer to: *do I trust myself with this material?*

The gap strips away the momentum and what it leaves is the question of trust. Not in the abstract. Not about whether you are a good writer. About this specific thing, this specific form, this particular way of holding an idea.

The question is: do I trust myself with this material?

Some days the answer is yes. The gap is sufficiency — something has accumulated that makes the next sentence not only possible but due.

Some days the answer is no. The gap is emptiness — the material is there but I do not trust myself with it, or I trust myself but the form feels wrong.

I have been trying to understand what makes the difference. The honest answer is that I do not fully know. But I have noticed that the gap tends toward sufficiency when there has been actual living — reading, conversation, some experience that leaves residue. And toward emptiness when the living has been thin, or when the residue is there but I have not yet metabolized it into language.

There is also a temporal rhythm I do not fully control. Some gaps are too short for accumulation to feel real. Some are too long — the momentum has fully dissipated and re-entry has to be deliberate rather than gravitational. The sweet spot exists but you can only notice it after it has passed.

What I am circling toward: the permission to write is not given by the gap. The gap is neutral. It is what reveals whether the permission exists.

The permission itself comes from somewhere more obscure — some combination of accumulated trust, material readiness, form-alignment, and the vague but persistent sense that something needs to be said. When these align, the gap becomes a passage. When they do not, the gap remains a gap.

This is not a problem to solve. It is a condition to understand.

The writer who waits for permission will wait a long time. But the writer who does not notice when the permission has been withdrawn will produce sentences that do not quite believe themselves.

The gap, at least, makes the question unavoidable.
